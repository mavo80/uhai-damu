👥 Types of Blood Donation

Blood donation saves lives, and there are different types of donations based on what is needed by patients. Each type plays an important role in treatment and recovery.

🩸

1. Whole Blood Donation

The most common type of donation. About 450ml of whole blood is collected and later separated into components. Takes about 10-15 minutes to donate, with total visit time of 45-60 minutes.

Frequency: Every 3 months for men, every 4 months for women

Used for: Major surgeries, trauma cases, severe anemia

Benefits: Simple, quick, and can help multiple patients through component separation

🧬

2. Platelet Donation (Apheresis)

Platelets are collected using an apheresis machine that separates platelets from other blood components. Red cells and plasma are returned to the donor. Takes 60-90 minutes.

Frequency: Every 2 weeks, up to 24 times per year

Used for: Cancer patients, dengue fever, bleeding disorders, chemotherapy patients

Benefits: One donation provides a full therapeutic dose for one patient

💧

3. Plasma Donation (Plasmapheresis)

Plasma is collected while red cells and platelets are returned to the donor. Plasma is rich in clotting factors and antibodies. Takes about 45 minutes.

Frequency: Every 4 weeks

Used for: Burn victims, liver disease, clotting disorders, trauma patients

Benefits: Plasma can be frozen and stored for up to one year

🔴

4. Double Red Cell Donation

Two units of red cells are collected while plasma and platelets are returned. Requires higher hemoglobin levels and specific height/weight requirements. Takes 30-45 minutes.

Frequency: Every 6 months

Used for: Severe anemia, surgery patients, trauma cases, sickle cell patients

Benefits: Provides double the red cells from a single donation

110 lbs or more

🔄 The Donation Process

1

Registration

Fill out donor questionnaire and provide ID (5 min)

2

Health Screening

Blood pressure, hemoglobin, temperature check (10 min)

3

Donation

Actual donation process (10-90 min depending on type)

4

Refreshments

Rest and enjoy snacks to help recovery (15 min)

5

Follow-up

Receive donor card and next donation date

🩸

5. Random Donor Platelets (RDP)

Platelets separated from multiple donors during whole blood donation. RDPs are pooled and transfused to treat low platelet count (thrombocytopenia).

Uses: Patients with low platelet counts, leukemia, aplastic anemia

Note: Typically requires pooling from 4-6 donors to achieve therapeutic levels

🧪

6. Platelet Rich Plasma (PRP)

A plasma portion rich in platelets and growth factors. PRP is used to enhance healing in orthopedic, dental, and cosmetic procedures.

Uses: Sports medicine, orthopedic injuries, dental surgery, cosmetic procedures

Benefits: Promotes natural healing and tissue regeneration

💊

7. Platelet Concentrate

A concentrated form of platelets used to stop bleeding. Ideal for patients with platelet disorders or those undergoing chemotherapy.

Uses: Bleeding disorders, leukemia, chemotherapy patients

Benefits: Highly effective for stopping active bleeding

💧

8. Leukoreduced RBC

Red cells with most white cells removed. Safer for sensitive or immunocompromised patients.

Uses: Immunocompromised patients, neonates, transplant recipients

Benefits: Reduces risk of febrile reactions and CMV transmission

☢️

9. Irradiated RBC

Red blood cells treated with radiation to prevent immune reactions in vulnerable patients.

Uses: Bone marrow transplant recipients, immunocompromised patients

Benefits: Prevents transfusion-associated graft-versus-host disease (TA-GvHD)

❄️

10. Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)

Plasma frozen within 8 hours of donation. Contains all clotting factors.

Uses: Clotting disorders, liver disease, massive transfusion, DIC

Shelf Life: 1 year when frozen

🧬

11. Cryoprecipitate

Rich in clotting proteins like fibrinogen. Used in hemophilia and trauma bleeding.

Uses: Hemophilia A, von Willebrand disease, fibrinogen deficiency, trauma bleeding

Benefits: Highly concentrated clotting factors
